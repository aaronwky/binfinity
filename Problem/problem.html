Limited Land Space for Waste Disposal

Singapore is a small and densely populated city-state with limited available land for waste disposal. It's land scarcity makes it challenging to find suitable locations for landfills or waste disposal facilities. Hence, the need for efficient use of available land has prompted the exploration of alternative waste management methods.

Fun Fact: Incinerated ash and non-incinerable waste are deposited to Semakau Landfill—the city-state’s sole landfill—which is projected to run out of space by 2035, or even earlier given the spike in waste generation amid the Covid-19 pandemic.

High Waste Generation, Low Recycling Rates

Singapore faces a significant challenge with high per capita waste generation, partially due to its affluent and consumer-driven society. This could be due to a culture of convenience and disposable products, contributing to environmental concerns. Despite efforts to promote recycling, the recycling rates in Singapore have historically been lower than desired.

Fun Fact: Despite awareness-raising campaigns to encourage a 3R (reduce, reuse and recycle) mindset, and designating 2019 as a ‘Year towards zero waste’, Singapore’s domestic recycling rate dropped from 22 per cent in 2018 to 17 per cent in 2019 – only 4 per cent of plastic and 18 per cent of food is recycled in the city-state.

Waste-to-energy initiatives are not sustainable in the long run

Waste-to-energy (WTE) initiatives involve the conversion of various types of waste materials into energy, typically in the form of incineration. However, WTE initiatives are not sustainable in the long run as they do not address the root cause of waste generation and could disincentivise recycling efforts. Furthermore, the incineration process releases harmful pollutants into the atmosphere, contributing to air pollution and potential health hazards. Balancing energy recovery with environmental considerations remains a key challenge for sustainable waste management.

Fun Fact: While more than 40% of Singapore's waste are incinerated, the energy generated from incineration only accounted for 3% of Singapore's electricity needs.

Contamination of Recycable Materials

Contamination of recyclable materials is a persistent issue in Singapore's recycling system. The lack of awareness and proper recycling practices (such as the need for a recyclable item to be washed or rinsed) by Singaporeans can lead to the mixing of recyclables with non-recyclables. This contamination hampers the efficiency of recycling processes and reduces the quality of recycled materials.

Fun Fact: According to a 2018 study by the Singapore Environment Council (SEC), 70% of respondents did not fully know which items were considered recyclable. In addition, 40% of trash placed into recycling bins are contaminated, and hence are considered as non-recycable wastes.

Regulations are not enough

While regulations and policies are in place to manage waste in Singapore, they are not sufficient to address the root causes of waste generation and to promote sustainable waste management practices. As stringent regulations on sustainablility may affect the economy, the government has to strike a balance between economic growth and environmental sustainability. This led to the slow progress in implementing more sustainable waste management practices.

Fun Fact: Minister for Sustainability and the Environment, Grace Fu, Singapore is not “in a hurry” to ban plastic bags because it burns waste to produce energy.
